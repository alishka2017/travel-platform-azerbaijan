Azerbaijan’s wine story begins in the Neolithic period – archaeological excavations in Goy‑Göl have uncovered grape pips and pottery wine jars dating to 5,000 BCE. For millennia, wine was produced in clay vessels (qəp) buried underground, a method still used today in some villages.

The Soviet era turned wine into a mass‑production industry, focusing on sweet fortified wines. Since independence, a new generation of winemakers is reviving ancient grape varieties and embracing modern techniques, putting Azerbaijani wine back on the world map.

## Indigenous Grape Varieties

Azerbaijan boasts over 400 native grape varieties, many of which grow nowhere else. The most important for wine are:

- **Madrasa** (red): The flagship red grape, producing medium‑bodied wines with notes of cherry, plum, and spice. Grown mainly in the Shamakhi region.
- **Bayanshira** (white): Aromatic, floral, with citrus and stone‑fruit flavors. Makes excellent dry whites and sparkling wines.
- **Xindogny** (red): Hardy, high‑acid grape used for both red wines and the traditional “şərab” (light table wine).
- **Gara Şireyi** (red): Deeply colored, tannic, used for robust aged reds.
- **Ağ Şireyi** (white): Neutral profile, often blended or used for brandy.

## Wine Regions to Explore

### 1. **Shamakhi Region**
The historic heart of Azerbaijani wine. Mountain vineyards at 600–800m altitude produce elegant, aromatic wines.

- **Meysəri Winery**: Family‑run estate focusing on Madrasa and Bayanshira. Their “Meysəri Madrasa” won gold at the Berlin Wine Trophy.
- **Şamaxı Şərab Zavodu**: The largest winery, offering tours and tastings of both traditional and modern styles.

### 2. **Ganja‑Goygöl Region**
German settlers in the 19th century brought European techniques here. The area is known for crisp whites and sparkling wines.

- **Goy‑Göl Winery**: Established in 1860 by German immigrants, this historic winery still uses original underground cellars. Try their “Goy‑Göl Bayanshira Brut”.
- **Ağstafa Wine Company**: Experimenting with organic viticulture and amphora aging.

### 3. **Nakhchivan Autonomous Republic**
A unique terroir with volcanic soils and extreme continental climate. Wines are intense, mineral‑driven.

- **Naxçıvan Şərab Zavodu**: Specializes in sweet dessert wines from sun‑dried grapes.
- **Ordubad micro‑region**: Tiny vineyards producing rare varietal wines from local grapes like “Qaraqılıq”.

### 4. **Tovuz Region** (Western Azerbaijan)
Bordering Georgia, this region shares similar winemaking traditions. Known for powerful reds.

- **Tovuz Şərab Zavodu**: Offers a “wine safari” through vineyards followed by a tasting in a Soviet‑era cellar.

## Traditional Winemaking: Qəp Method

In villages around Ismayilli, Gabala, and Sheki, winemakers still use the ancient qəp method:

1. Grapes are foot‑trodden in a stone trough (**şərab çənəsi**).
2. The juice, skins, and seeds ferment together in a large clay jar (qəp) buried up to its neck in the ground.
3. After primary fermentation, the qəp is sealed with a clay lid and left for 4–6 months.
4. The wine is then transferred to smaller qəps for aging (up to 5 years).

The result is a rustic, complex wine with earthy, oxidative notes. It’s an acquired taste but a direct link to antiquity.

## Modern Boutique Wineries

A new wave of small‑scale, quality‑focused wineries is emerging:

- **Fireland Wines** (Baku): Urban winery importing grapes from across Azerbaijan. Their “Sehirli” blend (Madrasa/Xindogny) has gained international attention.
- **Vinoteca** (Baku): Wine bar and micro‑winery producing natural wines with minimal intervention.
- **Chabiant** (Ganja): Focus on organic and biodynamic practices.

## Wine Tourism Experiences

- **Vineyard stays**: Several wineries offer guesthouses (Meysəri, Goy‑Göl).
- **Harvest festivals**: September–October in Shamakhi and Ganja.
- **Wine & food pairing**: Traditional Azerbaijani meze (cheese, nuts, dried fruits) pair beautifully with local wines.
- **Wine courses**: The Azerbaijan Sommelier Association offers introductory courses in Baku.

## How to Bring Wine Home

- **Limit**: 3 liters per person for EU/US (check your country’s regulations).
- **Packaging**: Most wineries offer sealed, travel‑safe bottles.
- **Shipping**: Some larger wineries can arrange international shipping.

## Beyond Wine: Other Grape Beverages

- **Şərab**: Light, low‑alcohol table wine consumed daily in rural areas.
- **Araq**: Grape brandy, often home‑distilled. The best comes from Nakhchivan.
- **Doshab**: Grape syrup reduced with flour and herbs, used in desserts and as a sweetener.

Azerbaijani wine is more than a drink – it’s a taste of history, terroir, and revival. Whether you’re a casual sipper or a serious oenophile, the vineyards of Azerbaijan offer a surprising and rewarding journey.
