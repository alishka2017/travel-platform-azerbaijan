Azerbaijani cuisine is often reduced to kebabs and plov (pilaf) in travel guides, but that’s like describing Italian food as just pizza and pasta. The country’s culinary tradition is one of the oldest and most diverse in the Caucasus, shaped by the Silk Road, seasonal abundance, and a deep‑rooted culture of hospitality.

## The Foundation: Bread, Herbs, and Dairy

No Azerbaijani meal begins without **çörək** (bread). The most beloved is **tandır çörəyi**, a round, crusty loaf baked in a clay tandir oven. It’s not just a side – it’s used to scoop food, wipe plates clean, and as an edible utensil. A meal without fresh bread feels incomplete.

Fresh herbs are another cornerstone. Markets overflow with **revan**, **göyərti**, **cebri**, and **şüyüd** (varieties of mint, cilantro, tarragon, and dill). Herbs aren’t just garnishes; they’re served in bunches as a side dish, folded into salads, and used as fillings for **qutab** (thin, savory turnovers).

Dairy appears in many forms: **qaymaq** (clotted cream), **pendir** (local feta‑style cheese), and **ayran** (yogurt drink). A typical breakfast includes bread, honey, qaymaq, and tea – a simple yet perfect combination.

## Regional Specialties You Must Try

### From the Mountains: **Qutab**
These thin, crescent‑shaped pastries are cooked on a saj (convex griddle). Fillings vary by season: spring brings wild greens (**göyərti qutabı**), summer offers pumpkin (**balqabaq qutabı**), and meat versions are year‑round. Eat them hot, brushed with butter, and sprinkled with sumac.

### From the Caspian Coast: **Balıq Plov**
While plov is universal, the coastal version uses **sturgeon**, **kutum**, or **salmon** instead of meat. The fish is grilled separately and served atop fragrant saffron rice with dried fruits and chestnuts. The key is **əzgil** (barberry) for a touch of acidity.

### From Sheki: **Şəki pitisi**
A unique soup‑stew served in individual clay pots. Layers of mutton, chickpeas, chestnuts, and potatoes are slow‑cooked for hours. Just before serving, a lump of sheep‑tail fat is placed on top to melt into the broth. It’s rich, hearty, and a cold‑weather favorite.

### From Nakhchivan: **Düşbərə**
Tiny lamb‑filled dumplings in a clear broth, flavored with mint and vinegar. They’re so small (about the size of a fingertip) that making them is a test of patience and skill. Traditionally served after midnight during Novruz celebrations.

## The Art of Plov

Yes, plov deserves its own section because there are over 40 documented varieties in Azerbaijan. The basics: long‑grain rice, saffron, and butter. Beyond that, creativity reigns.

- **Şah plov** (“Royal plov”): Decorated with grilled quail, dried fruits, nuts, and egg slices.
- **Toyuq plov**: Chicken plov with raisins and dried apricots.
- **Səbzi qovurma plov**: Rice topped with stewed meat and herbs.

Every region claims the best plov, but the most respected masters (**aşpazlar**) come from Lankaran and Sheki.

## Modern Interpretations

Baku’s dining scene has seen a revival of traditional dishes presented with contemporary flair. Restaurants like **Chinar**, **Şirvanşah**, and **Mugam Club** offer updated versions of classics: frothy **dovğa** (yogurt soup) served chilled in a glass, deconstructed **pakhlava** with salted‑caramel ice cream, and **lavangi** (stuffed chicken or fish) with molecular garnishes.

## How to Experience Real Azerbaijani Cuisine

1. **Visit a local market** (like Teze Bazar in Baku) to see ingredients in their raw glory. Talk to vendors – they’ll often share recipe tips.
2. **Accept home invitations**. Azerbaijanis are famously hospitable; if someone invites you for a meal, say yes. You’ll experience dishes never found in restaurants.
3. **Take a cooking class**. Several Baku‑based chefs offer classes in plov, qutab, and bread‑making.
4. **Eat seasonally**. Spring is for fresh herbs and lamb; summer for tomatoes and fish; autumn for pomegranates and nuts; winter for preserved foods and hearty stews.

## Recipe: Simple Herb Qutab

*Ingredients:*
- 2 cups flour
- 1 cup water
- 1 tsp salt
- 2 cups chopped fresh herbs (cilantro, dill, mint, spinach)
- 1 small onion, finely chopped
- Salt, pepper
- Butter for brushing

*Method:*
1. Mix flour, water, and salt to form a soft dough. Rest 30 minutes.
2. Divide dough into 8 balls. Roll each into a thin circle.
3. Mix herbs, onion, salt, pepper. Spread filling on half of each circle.
4. Fold over, seal edges, cook on a hot, unoiled skillet until browned on both sides.
5. Brush with butter and serve immediately.
